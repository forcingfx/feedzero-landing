Eating Our Own Dog Food

You're reading this in FeedZero's own release notes feed. That's new. We deleted the old changelog dialog — 700 lines of custom UI — and replaced it with a real Atom feed at https://feedzero.app/releases.xml. Any RSS reader can subscribe to it. We subscribe to it ourselves.

The first time you open FeedZero, it automatically adds this feed. If you removed it, you can get it back from Settings → "What's new." It works like any other feed — unread badges, mark as read, the whole thing.

Clear Cached Articles

The three-dot menu on every feed now has "Clear cached articles." It does what it says: deletes all stored articles for that feed and re-fetches from the source. Useful when a feed updates its content (like, say, when we rewrite these release notes three times in one day).

There's a confirmation dialog because this is destructive — your read/unread status is gone, and older articles might not come back if the feed no longer publishes them.

Headings Work Now

Article content now renders h1–h4 headings with proper sizing and spacing. Lists get markers. It sounds basic — it was. We also fixed it.

Feeds Are Sorted

The sidebar used to show feeds in whatever order IndexedDB felt like returning them. Now it's alphabetical, with the release notes feed pinned to the top. Small thing, but it was bothering us.

The Tracking Problem

Here's something most people don't realize: RSS feeds are full of tracking junk. Invisible 1×1 pixel images that ping Facebook and Google every time you open an article. Links stuffed with utm_source and fbclid parameters so the publisher can track exactly which link you clicked and where you came from.

RSS was supposed to be the open, decentralized alternative. Instead it became another vector for the same surveillance apparatus that ruined the web. So we decided to do something about it.

Starting with this release, FeedZero strips all of it out before the content reaches your browser:

🔍
Tracking pixels
1×1 images from Facebook, Google Analytics, Quantserve, Feedburner, and friends
🔗
URL tracking parameters
utm_source, utm_medium, utm_campaign, fbclid, gclid, and 20+ others
🛡️
Ad click IDs
Microsoft, Snapchat, Twitter, Pinterest click tracking — all of it

Here's what a typical link looks like before and after:

BEFORE
https://example.com/article?utm_source=rss&utm_medium=feed&utm_campaign=spring&fbclid=abc123
AFTER
https://example.com/article

You don't have to do anything. It just happens.

The Feed Catalog

We also started building something interesting on the server side. When anyone fetches a feed, we now record that the feed exists — but not who fetched it. No user IDs, no sessions, no cookies.

Think of it this way: the server knows "BBC News is a feed that exists and gets fetched a lot." It has absolutely no idea that you read BBC News. That distinction matters, and it's the foundation for everything we want to build next — feed health monitoring, popularity data, maybe recommendations. All without ever knowing who reads what.

Cloud Sync

Here's how sync works: you get a four-word passphrase. That passphrase is used to derive an encryption key. Your feeds and article metadata are encrypted with that key and uploaded as an opaque blob. The server has no idea what's inside.

oak sun fox bell

Type those four words on another device and your feeds appear. That's it. No account, no email, no password reset flow. If you lose the passphrase, the data is gone — by design.

This release also adds OPML import and export, unread indicators, and mark-all-read.

Hello, World

Google killed Google Reader in 2013. That was over a decade ago. In the time since, RSS didn't die — it just stopped being convenient. Every reader that popped up to fill the void either got acqui-hired into oblivion, pivoted to "AI-powered news curation" (read: tracking), or just quietly started selling your reading habits to advertisers.

FeedZero is the reader I wanted to exist. You paste a URL, you read the articles. That's it. No account. No email. No "Sign in with Google." The whole thing runs in your browser, and everything is encrypted before it touches disk.

Here's the actual data flow:

1 You add a feed URL
2 Content fetched through our proxy (publisher sees our server, not you)
3 HTML sanitized — no scripts, no XSS, no funny business
4 Encrypted with AES-256 right in your browser
5 Stored locally in IndexedDB — never leaves your machine unencrypted

This first version handles RSS 2.0, Atom, and JSON Feed. There's dark mode, vim-style keyboard navigation, and a full-text extractor for feeds that only ship summaries.
